Overview overview Overview

Reimagining the Sketch* product experience was my first ever large-scale client project and one that will always have a place in my heart. In 2018, I was the sole designer at the software agency I was working. I joined the project from the beginning and continued until it ended one year later. I've always liked exploring new ways of collaboration and diving deeper into brainstorming experiments, which was the main reason I was chosen to join the team, as my company was also open to experimentation at that time.

ACI Sketch is a web and mobile application used by appraisers and property inspectors in the US, and it consists of two parts: the floor plan sketching and data collection. It allows property inspectors to look for properties, schedule appointments for inspection, collect data, outline accurate floor plans and manage the paperwork around the appraisal process.

Even though Sketch had been distributed to its customers and employees for many years, there had been significant shifts to competitive products over time. Eventually, our clients approached my agency, intending to rebrand and improve the user experience of their product but also rewrite the codebase from scratch.

    Since we had the opportunity to redesign the whole experience from scratch, we needed to identify all significant usability issues that existed already, so we would avoid similar patterns in our future solution. After an agreement with our client, I suggested to my team to conduct a short UX audit to study the inspector's workflow in-depth and understand at which step of the flow their problems might occur. We happily dove into it. We each tried to create a floor plan for our apartment or part of our company's layout. The result was expected among us:

    • The sketch tools malfunctioned, mainly because of bugs, and didn't synergize together to ensure a pleasant experience. What seemed easy to do in the beginning ended up taking us a lot of time and effort.
    • Switching between the sketch and form view could have been more intuitive. We needed help locating the exact input fields that had to be filled, as the form was too long and unstructured.

    Now that we had our priorities in place, I decided to tackle the problems one by one:

    1. Research form best practices and prepare a list of tips to keep in mind when redesigning the long legal form in the future, trying to figure out how to make the navigation simple and the hierarchy flat.

     exploration The first concepts of sketching

    Studying different sketching techniques was one of the most enjoyable parts of our research. We knew sketching using a Numpad was an industry standard, but we also wanted to introduce more freedom when editing shapes. So I came up with 3 different concepts:

    1. Sketching with a Numpad (current solution)
    2. Sketching with predefined shapes
    3. Freehand sketching
    Early sketching concepts

    The 2nd concept was discarded because it didn't reflect the inspector's natural workflow. For example, inspectors first measure a wall's length, then draw the line on the app with the Numpad (e.g., by typing 10' â†’ Enter), continuing until they have a closed shape. Drawing with predefined shapes would go against that flow and would require more editing while on the field, which is something we were trying to avoid.

    We chose freehand sketching and decided to focus more on the desktop experience, allowing inspectors to manually create or edit shapes using a mouse, something previously impossible.

    design sprint Solving the most crucial problem was a team effort

    Meanwhile, a 4-day Design Sprint was planned to solve the problem of sketch/form communication. This was my first Design Sprint, an experience that inspired me to dive deeper into workshop facilitation techniques. I won't go through all the workshop's details; I'll only mention that I'd consider it successful. Allocating time doing UX research allowed us to save time, be informed, ask better questions and narrow down the challenge into smaller chunks.

    We managed to come up with a solution for our most critical challenge but also generated many other exciting concepts on inspection management, data form navigation, and data collection:

    • Ability to manage, filter, and manually schedule inspection orders
    • A "Split View" concept that would partially display the legal form as well as the sketching area and a quick transition between the views by a drag gesture.
    • Selecting an area label (e.g., kitchen), zooming into it, and automatically transitioning to the corresponding section on the legal form
    • Displaying complete/incomplete status on area labels
    Our concept produced during the Design Sprint

    Due to time limitations and since our clients wanted to be present during the testing sessions, we recruited 4 participants internally. This wasn't a major blocker, as the goal of the testing was to observe how people perceived the sketch-form transition and the concept overall, so no prior experience with property inspection was necessary.

    Sketching the exterior

    Adding points to an edge

    By selecting an edge, users could add multiple points to it. Similarly to sketch mode, a corner (point) would start following the mouse to assist in aiming. By clicking anywhere on the canvas, users returned to select mode.

    Adding points to an edge

    Extrusion and creating curved walls

    I designed the corners and the edges so that they would slightly change in color on hover and become blue-ish when selected. I added two small arrows around the edge to indicate that it can be moved (locked in one direction). Just like adding points, by selecting an edge, users could turn it into a curve of a predefined SOMETHING, that could be edited. We knew that inspectors always measured the SOMETHING to calculate the curve.

    Extrusion and creating curved walls

    Adding interior walls

    Interior walls worked in the same way as the exterior, without the need to have closed shapes. I learned during research that inspectors draw diagonal lines by only measuring the A and B, so I designed dashed lines to show their dimensions.

    Adding interior walls

    Adding labels, symbols and object transformation

    I thought a dragging gesture would be appropriate to add objects to the canvas so that the behavior would work on tablets more naturally as well. A selected object would be marked with a blue outline and squared corners, helping in scaling and rotation, along with changes in the cursor for additional aid.

    Adding labels, symbols and object transformation     pre-release testing A round of usability testing before beta release

    Right before the beta release, I performed another round of usability testing. The goal was to test the overall perception of the left toolbar, the ability to sketch and edit an actual floor plan, delete objects and zoom in/out or move around the canvas. For a realistic experience, we used an instance of the development branch instead of a prototype, and we tested 6 people using the usertesting.com platform to experiment with unmoderated testing. After analyzing the videos and organizing all the data, I found that the following challenges stood out:

    1. Some of the side menu icons were not fully understood. The development instance that was used lacked the implementation of tooltips, as suggested in the prototypes, but that could easily be fixed.
    2. All participants had trouble stopping sketching. The current behavior enforced clicking on the select item at the side menu, which was far away from the sketching area. As tooltips and shortcuts still needed to be implemented, users mentioned that it would feel more natural to use the ESC key to cancel and return to select mode.
    3. Some participants tried to hover over an edge and immediately drag to move it without it first being selected. To solve this, we tried out a concept where the small arrows around the edges would appear on hover instead of selection, and all the objects on the canvas would be draggable without them having to be selected first.
